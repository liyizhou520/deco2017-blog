This week focused on testing, analytics, and evaluating whether a design actually works for users.

Compared with previous weeks that focused heavily on building systems and features, this tutorial encouraged us to step back and critically evaluate the effectiveness of our decisions.

One important idea from the lecture was that designs are never truly finished.

A website continues evolving as long as users continue interacting with it. Because of this, testing should not be treated as a final step that only happens after development ends.

The lecture introduced multiple forms of testing, including:

- User testing
- Integration testing
- Accessibility testing
- Analytics
- Automated testing

What stood out most to me was the idea of choosing “the cheapest test that answers the question”.

For example:

- Paper sketches can test layout ideas
- Wireframes can test navigation flow
- HTML prototypes can test interaction
- Analytics can reveal long-term user behaviour

This changed how I think about prototyping. Previously, I often assumed more polished prototypes automatically produced better testing results. Now I realise simpler methods are often more efficient during early stages.

Another major topic was analytics and ethical data collection.

The lecture explained that collecting data simply because it is possible is not a good approach. Instead, designers should first decide what question they are trying to answer.

This made me think more carefully about responsibility in digital systems.

If our project eventually tracks user behaviour, we should consider:

- What information is actually necessary
- Whether users understand what is being collected
- How privacy is protected
- Whether the data collection is ethical

I found the discussion about “anonymous” data especially interesting because even anonymous datasets can sometimes identify individuals once combined with enough information.

This week also encouraged me to reflect on our project overall.

Compared with the beginning of the semester, I now think much more carefully about:

- Structure
- User flow
- Data relationships
- Error handling
- Testing
- Scalability

At the start, I mainly viewed websites as visual interfaces. Now I understand they are complex systems combining design, logic, interaction, and infrastructure together.

Although there are still many technical skills I need to improve, I feel much more confident understanding the overall development process than I did at the beginning of the semester.
