This week felt like an important transition point for the project.

Previously, most of our work existed inside sketches and wireframes. Now we needed to translate those ideas into actual HTML pages that users could interact with.

At first I assumed this process would be straightforward because we already had the layouts prepared in Figma.

It was not.

One of the biggest lessons this week was realising that something which looks organised inside a wireframe may become much more complicated once implemented in code.

For example, the Sound Room cards on our homepage originally looked simple in Figma, but while building them in HTML I had to think carefully about:

- Repeated structures
- Content hierarchy
- Headings and spacing
- Navigation links
- Responsive layout behaviour

Some layouts that looked perfectly fine visually suddenly became awkward once real content was inserted.

We also performed a small informal user test during tutorial time. We showed our wireframes to several classmates and observed how they interpreted the interface.

This was surprisingly useful.

Certain parts that seemed “obvious” to us were actually unclear to other people. For example, some users did not immediately understand what a Sound Room represented or where they were expected to click.

That feedback changed how I viewed design.

I realised users do not see the interface with the same understanding as the creators. Designers already know the structure beforehand, but users encounter everything for the first time.

Because of this, we simplified several areas of the homepage and made navigation paths more direct.

After that, I started building sections in HTML more confidently.

However, coding also introduced technical frustrations. Sometimes very small mistakes in HTML structure caused layout problems that took a long time to solve.

At one point I spent a large amount of time trying to understand why certain card layouts refused to stay aligned properly.

Although frustrating, these problems helped me become more careful with structure and organisation.

By the end of the week, our project still looked incomplete, but for the first time it felt like a real website instead of only a concept.

That made the progress feel much more motivating.
